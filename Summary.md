# **Chapter 2- The Development of English**
Introduction

The chapter The Development of English examines both the historical evolution of English and the broader mechanisms of language change. It is explained that studying a language can be approached from two perspectives: synchronic and diachronic. A synchronic study focuses on a language as it exists at a particular moment in time, while a diachronic study examines how a language changes across history. English is one of these languages that has undergone several major historical stages, commonly divided into: Old English, Middle English, Early Modern English, Modern English, Contemporary English. These stages reflect the linguistic transformations that occurred due to historical, social, and political influences. 
One of the most important events mentioned is the Norman Conquest of 1066, which introduced strong French influence into English vocabulary and contributed to the transition from Old English to Middle English. Similarly other languages are also not static systems but constantly changing entities. Language change occurs because of both: External influences, such as cultural and political developments and internal influences, such as natural grammatical simplification and regularization. An example of external change is the growing preference for gender-neutral vocabulary in English, while an example of internal change is the replacement of irregular verb forms with more regular forms, such as strived replacing strove. 
The introduction also highlights the global spread of English. Originally limited to England during the Old English period, English eventually developed into an international language spoken across the world. This worldwide expansion became one of the defining characteristics of Contemporary English.

The Current State of the English Language

This chapter focuses on the modern global position of English and the difficulties involved in defining and counting languages and their speakers. According to ‘Ethnologue: Languages of the World’, there are approximately 6,900 living languages worldwide. Although many of these languages are spoken by relatively small populations, a limited number of languages dominate internationally. English is among the most widely spoken languages in the world and has become a major global language used for communication in politics, business, education, science, and media. 

The spread of English is largely connected to historical and geopolitical developments rather than to any inherent superiority of the language itself. The two most significant factors were: The expansion of the British Empire through colonization and the rise of the United States as a major political and economic power in the twentieth century Because of this expansion, English today has more non-native speakers than native speakers. It functions as an international lingua franca, allowing communication between speakers from different backgrounds. 

The chapter also addressed many important linguistic problems, an example of which is the distinction between a language and a dialect is often not purely linguistic but also political and cultural. In many cases, varieties considered separate languages may be mutually intelligible, while some dialects of the same language may differ significantly. Therefore, defining what counts as a “language” can be highly subjective and influenced by national identity, politics, and social prestige. 

Another issue discussed is the difficulty of calculating the number of speakers of a language. Many people are bilingual or multilingual, making it complicated to determine whether someone should be counted as a first-language speaker, second-language speaker, or both. These complications are especially relevant in the case of English because of its widespread use in international communication and education. 
The whole section demonstrated the modern position of English which is the result of centuries of historical development, political power, and cultural influence. English is no longer simply the language of England or even native English-speaking countries; it has become a global means of communication used by millions of people worldwide in both native and non-native contexts.


The first part of the chapter outlines the origins of the English language, which date back to around 400 AD, when, following the withdrawal of the Romans, Britain began to be settled by Germanic tribes (the Angles, Saxons, and Jutes). They displaced the indigenous Celts, establishing a language known as Old English (Anglo-Saxon), which was characterised by an extensive inflectional system, including declension by case, number, and gender. During this period, English was spoken almost exclusively, and writing developed only after the Christianisation of the island in the 6th century, thanks to the monks.

Transition to the Middle English period was initiated by the Norman conquest in 1066. For around 300 years, England was ruled by the Normans, who spoke a variant of French called Anglo-Norman, which led to a massive influx of French vocabulary into the English language. At the same time, the inflectional endings continued to disappear, making Middle English more similar to its modern form. Although these changes were drastic, scholars regard them as a natural process of linguistic evolution through contact with another language.

The next section focuses on the emergence of the early modern language, heralded by the linguistic phenomenon known as the Great Vowel Shift. This involved a change in the articulation of long vowels, which began to be pronounced ‘higher’ or transformed into diphthongs. The standardisation of the language at that time was significantly influenced by the introduction of printing by Caxton in 1476, which increased literacy levels, and by publications such as Tyndale’s Bible, the King James Bible, and the works of Shakespeare.

The process of codifying the English language was reinforced in the 18th century by the publication of groundbreaking dictionaries by Samuel Johnson and Noah Webster, as well as grammars such as Robert Lowth’s work. The final key stage was the colonisation of America in the 17th century, which marked the beginning of the language’s global expansion, leading to the emergence of wide new varieties of English and the consolidation of the American variety’s dominant position in the world.

## Typological Classifications of Languages
&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The subchapter begins with comparison of two ways of classifying languages. The first one is the comparative method, which draws on both linguistic and non-linguistic evidence. The second one is the typological method, which focuses only on linguistic features. Although phonological features are also used in classification, most of the research in this area has centered on morphology and syntax.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;When morphology is considered, languages are grouped into three categories. The first one is agglutinative languages, such as Turkish, where words are built up by putting together easily identifiable morphemes. For instance, in the Turkish sentence “eve gitmek istiyorum,” every word can be picked apart and each morpheme identified. The opposite of agglutinative languages is isolating languages, such as Mandarin Chinese. In these languages, words stand alone as independent units. Moreover, grammatical relationships are expressed through separate words rather than affixes. The third category are fusional languages, which include Latin, German, and Russian. In these languages, grammatical information such as case, number, and gender can be expressed through a single morpheme. In addition, some languages do not fit into just one category. For instance, over centuries English has been moving from largely fusional towards a more isolating pattern. 

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Considering syntax, languages can also be classified by word order, meaning how the subject, verb, and object are arranged in a sentence. Tomlin's survey of 402 languages found that, when put together, SOV and SVO word order appear in 87% of all languages studied. For instance, English is a SVO language and Japanese is a SOV language. Moreover, this subchapter introduces the idea of markedness. It is the difference between what is considered normal (unmarked) and what is considered unusual (marked) in a language. In English, the SVO word order is unmarked, as it is the most used word order and sentences with it generally do not require context. In contrast, OSV word order is marked, meaning it requires context to be acceptable. Furthermore, languages with complex case systems, such as Russian, allow for more flexible word order. This is possible because grammatical roles in this language are already signaled by affixes rather than by position in the sentence, unlike in English. Similarly, in German, word order varies depending on clause type. In main clauses, the dominant word order is SVO, whereas in subordinate clauses a grammatical rule requires all parts of the verb phrase to appear at the end, resulting in SOV word order. Accordingly, it can be hard to assign a dominant word order to languages with flexible word order.

&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;The final section of the subchapter addresses the link between typology and language universals, presenting two opposing approaches. On the one hand, generative linguists, following Chomsky’s theory, believe children are born with a set of universal language principles. According to this group, learning a language is a matter of setting certain parameters based on surrounding linguistic input. An example of such parameters is the pro-drop parameter. It determines whether a language allows the subject pronoun to be dropped, as in Spanish, or requires it to be included, as in English. On the other hand, other typologists, such as Greenberg and Tomlin, prefer to survey large samples of real languages and draw conclusions from statistics. For this group, evidence of universal tendency is, for instance, the dominance of SOV and SVO orders across various languages.
