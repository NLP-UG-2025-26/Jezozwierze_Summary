Typological Classifications of Languages
The subchapter begins with comparison of two ways of classifying languages. The first one is the comparative method, which draws on both linguistic and non-linguistic evidence. The second one is the typological method, which focuses only on linguistic features. Although phonological features are also used in classification, most of the research in this area has centered on morphology and syntax.
When morphology is considered, languages are grouped into three categories. The first one is agglutinative languages, such as Turkish, where words are built up by putting together easily identifiable morphemes. For instance, in the Turkish sentence “eve gitmek istiyorum,” every word can be picked apart and each morpheme identified. The opposite of agglutinative languages is isolating languages, such as Mandarin Chinese. In these languages, words stand alone as independent units. Moreover, grammatical relationships are expressed through separate words rather than affixes. The third category are fusional languages, which include Latin, German, and Russian. In these languages, grammatical information such as case, number, and gender can be expressed through a single morpheme. In addition, some languages do not fit into just one category. For instance, over centuries English has been moving from largely fusional towards a more isolating pattern. 
Considering syntax, languages can also be classified by word order, meaning how the subject, verb, and object are arranged in a sentence. Tomlin's survey of 402 languages found that, when put together, SOV and SVO word order appear in 87% of all languages studied. For instance, English is a SVO language and Japanese is a SOV language. Moreover, this subchapter introduces the idea of markedness. It is the difference between what is considered normal (unmarked) and what is considered unusual (marked) in a language. In English, the SVO word order is unmarked, as it is the most used word order and sentences with it generally do not require context. In contrast, OSV word order is marked, meaning it requires context to be acceptable. Furthermore, languages with complex case systems, such as Russian, allow for more flexible word order. This is possible because grammatical roles in this language are already signaled by affixes rather than by position in the sentence, unlike in English. Similarly, in German, word order varies depending on clause type. In main clauses, the dominant word order is SVO, whereas in subordinate clauses a grammatical rule requires all parts of the verb phrase to appear at the end, resulting in SOV word order. Accordingly, it can be hard to assign a dominant word order to languages with flexible word order.
The final section of the subchapter addresses the link between typology and language universals, presenting two opposing approaches. On the one hand, generative linguists, following Chomsky’s theory, believe children are born with a set of universal language principles. According to this group, learning a language is a matter of setting certain parameters based on surrounding linguistic input. An example of such parameters is the pro-drop parameter. It determines whether a language allows the subject pronoun to be dropped, as in Spanish, or requires it to be included, as in English. On the other hand, other typologists, such as Greenberg and Tomlin, prefer to survey large samples of real languages and draw conclusions from statistics. For this group, evidence of universal tendency is, for instance, the dominance of SOV and SVO orders across various languages.
