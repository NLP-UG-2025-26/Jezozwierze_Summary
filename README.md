Why Languages Change
Linguists have long sought to explain why languages change — and why related languages, descended from the same ancestral tongue, can evolve so differently. English and German both derive from Proto-Germanic, yet German has preserved an elaborate inflectional system marking case, number, and gender, while English has shed most of this morphological complexity. Understanding such divergence requires examining influences that are partly internal to language structure and partly external, arising from social, political, and cultural circumstances.
Many linguists use the terms 'change' and 'evolution' interchangeably. In everyday usage, 'evolution' has become a loose synonym for development or improvement, and this metaphorical sense has carried into linguistics. Some scholars, however, go further. William Croft (2000) proposes a formal 'Theory of utterance selection' grounded in Hull's (1988) generalized theory of selection, which extends Darwinian principles beyond biology to encompass all evolutionary phenomena, including language change.
In Croft's framework, every act of communication is an opportunity for linguistic transmission. When speakers use a conventional form and their interlocutors replicate it, this constitutes 'normal replication.' When a speaker deviates from convention, it constitutes 'altered replication.' The ongoing replacement of whom by who in English illustrates this well. Historically, 'Whom do you trust?' was the correct form; at some point, speakers began producing 'Who do you trust?' — an altered replication. Today both forms coexist, with 'whom' carrying a more formal register. This instability also produces hypercorrection: speakers aware of 'whom' as a prestige form sometimes deploy it incorrectly, as in 'She's the person whom I believe is in charge,' where 'whom' actually occupies a subject, not an object, position. Over time, Croft predicts, 'whom' will disappear entirely. Many linguists, however, remain skeptical of pressing the biological analogy too far, noting that linguistic change has adequate explanations in internal and external influences alone.
The traditional approach distinguishes between internally and externally motivated change. Internal changes arise from systematic mechanisms built into the structure of language itself — they would occur even if a language were sealed off from all outside contact. English's long transition from a fusional to an isolating language is a prime example. Whaley (1997) describes a cycle through which all languages move over time: from isolating to agglutinative to fusional, and back again. English is currently in the morphological-loss phase of this cycle. Another classic internal change is the Great Vowel Shift (roughly the 14th–17th centuries), which Labov (1994) classifies as a chain shift: a cascading realignment of vowels in which some vowels raised or became diphthongs, pushing others out of position. Such patterns recur across many languages, suggesting that phonological architecture generates its own pressures independent of social context.
External changes, by contrast, result from language contact. Thomason and Kaufman (1988) propose a 'borrowing scale' ranging from casual contact to extreme cultural pressure. At the low end, speakers borrow vocabulary to fill lexical gaps. During the Old English period, the introduction of Christianity brought Latin words such as mass, abbot, and candle into English — borrowings that named new concepts without altering the grammar. Higher up the scale, structural borrowings occur: the Latin prefix sub- entered English and was eventually attached to native Germanic words, yielding hybrids like postgame. Even inflections were sometimes transferred: the Latin forms medium/media and datum/data entered English with their original endings, though today media is widely reanalyzed as a singular collective noun — an illustration of how borrowed structures eventually conform to the logic of their new linguistic environment.
The most extreme consequence of sustained external pressure is language death — the complete extinction of a living tongue. Language death occurs when successive generations abandon a language until no fluent speakers remain. Latin is often cited as a dead language, but its legacy survives in its descendants: Spanish, French, Italian, Portuguese, and others. The deaths linguists find most devastating are those that leave no descendants — languages simply extinguished, taking with them unique ways of encoding history and experience.
In the Americas, colonization, genocide, epidemic disease, and the dominance of European languages combined to eradicate or endanger hundreds of indigenous languages. Of the forty-four languages in the Algic family, thirteen have already died. Even Navajo, which counted nearly 150,000 speakers in the 1990 census, has seen a sharp generational decline: 90% of ethnically Navajo first-graders spoke it natively in 1968; today only 30% do. Revitalization efforts have been uneven. Welsh stands as a rare success, with about 19% of the Welsh population now able to speak the language, thanks to sustained political advocacy and Welsh-medium education. Most other efforts have struggled, since restoring intergenerational transmission requires not just documentation but a fundamental shift in the social conditions that caused speakers to abandon the language in the first place.
Crystal (2000) argues that each language encodes a unique interpretation of human experience, and its extinction is therefore an irreplaceable loss — not only for the community whose identity is bound up in it, but for linguistic science itself, which depends on the diversity of the world's languages to understand the full range of possible human grammars. The spread of English as a global language sharpens these concerns: while English provides unparalleled access to international networks of communication, its social and economic prestige has accelerated the abandonment of local languages worldwide, leading some scholars to describe it as a 'killer language.'
The Nature of Language Change
Language change is natural, normal, and ultimately inevitable. What prescriptivists describe as 'errors' or 'corruptions' are often the early stages of change in progress. Meyer illustrates this through several cases in contemporary English.
The confusion between flaunt and flout is a telling example. Flout means 'to violate a rule'; flaunt means 'to display ostentatiously.' The two words are phonetically similar, and flaunt is considerably more common in everyday use. In 1979, President Jimmy Carter, speaking about the Iran hostage crisis, used flaunt where flout was required — an error that gained notoriety precisely because it came from such a public and educated speaker, revealing how far the semantic distinction had already eroded. A similar collapse is underway with disinterested and uninterested: traditionally, disinterested means 'impartial,' while uninterested means 'lacking interest,' but increasingly the two are treated as synonyms. In both cases, a form once stigmatized as incorrect gradually achieves acceptance through sheer frequency of use, until it becomes the new standard.
The rise of singular they illustrates a different dimension of change: the pressure to fill a genuine structural gap. Traditional prescriptive grammar required the generic masculine he ('Everybody must bring his textbook'), but growing awareness of gender bias made this increasingly untenable. Proposed alternatives such as 'his or her' or 'he or she' are grammatically unimpeachable but stylistically cumbersome. Speakers have widely converged instead on singular they ('Everybody is trying their hardest'), which fills the gap left by the absence of a gender-neutral singular third-person pronoun. Prescriptivists objected on grounds of number agreement, but the solution's practical elegance has driven its adoption.
Notably, attempts to introduce an entirely new pronoun — such as the proposed ter — have consistently failed. Pronouns form a closed class, deeply embedded in grammar and acquired very early in language development; introducing a genuinely new pronoun is extraordinarily difficult. The repurposing of they sidesteps this obstacle by extending an already familiar form, making the change simultaneously conservative in means and innovative in function.
